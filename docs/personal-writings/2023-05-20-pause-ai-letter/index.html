A Six-Month Pause in Artificial Intelligence Research: Fear and Controversy

manifold.press
artificial intelligence
My personal opinion about the Future of Life Institute’s open letter demanding a six-month pause in AI research.
Author

Ömer Sümer

Published

May 20, 2023

We know that ideas, new inventions, and scientific discoveries were banned at different times and places in history. In fact, without going back in history, we often witness the prevention of access to information and technological tools in our country today. But beyond tools, what about the idea of “banning research and development”? Last month, an open letter was published proposing a 6-month halt to the development of AI models that are more powerful than GPT-4 (I consider this an “audit” or a “ban”). In this article, I will cover this letter and, more generally, the potential risks and dangers of AI-based tools.

The Future of Life Institute (FLI), which published the letter, is a non-profit research institute founded in 2014 in the US. Its mission is to reduce global catastrophic and existential risks from powerful technologies. It publishes reports that address technology-related problems and aim to formulate policy. Its advisors include popular names, including Elon Musk. An open letter 1 was published by FLI on March 29, and by the end of a month, it had been endorsed by over thirty thousand people.

“Contemporary AI systems are now becoming human-competitive at general tasks, and we must ask ourselves: Should we let machines flood our information channels with propaganda and untruth? Should we automate away all the jobs, including the fulfilling ones? Should we develop nonhuman minds that might eventually outnumber, outsmart, obsolete and replace us? Should we risk loss of control of our civilization? Such decisions must not be delegated to unelected tech leaders. Powerful AI systems should be developed only once we are confident that their effects will be positive and their risks will be manageable. This confidence must be well justified and increase with the magnitude of a system’s potential effects. OpenAI’s recent statement regarding artificial general intelligence, states that”At some point, it may be important to get independent review before starting to train future systems, and for the most advanced efforts to agree to limit the rate of growth of compute used for creating new models.” We agree. That point is now.
Therefore, we call on all AI labs to immediately pause for at least 6 months the training of AI systems more powerful than GPT-4. This pause should be public and verifiable, and include all key actors. If such a pause cannot be enacted quickly, governments should step in and institute a moratorium.”

Signatories of the letter include prominent figures. Yoshua Bengio, a deep learning researcher who received the prestigious ACM A.M. Turing Award in 2018; Gary Markus, professor emeritus of psychology at New York University and entrepreneur Gary Markus, who also founded technology companies; Yuval Noah H. H., an Israeli historian whose books “Sapiens” and “Homo Deus” have been widely translated into Turkish.

In the past month, there has been a debate between those who support the letter, those who support it but find it insufficient, and those who oppose it. This debate has intensified because, as the letter points out, large language models perform amazingly well on many tasks. This is because large language models like ChatGPT also fabricate information that is not real (also called hallucinations). This opens the door to different dangers, such as fake news and disinformation, in the use of big language models in daily life.

According to the website of OpenAI, which develops GPT models 2, they are aware of the alignment problem. Alignment refers to the organization of artificial intelligence systems in line with human goals, preferences, and ethical principles. OpenAI stated last year that it would approach this problem in three particular ways: Training AI using human feedback (reinforcement learning), training AI systems to assist human evaluation, and training AI systems to conduct adaptation research.

The potential dangers have become more visible in a few months as big language models have spread to different audiences and reached millions of users. However, even though big tech companies like OpenAI, DeepMind, Google, and Meta are developing AI-based tools and are aware of the potential risks, it is still hard to say that they have a clear plan.

Geoffrey Hinton, one of the trio of so-called “GodFathers of AI” (the other two, Yoshua Bengio and Yann LeCun, made significant contributions to today’s deep learning methods), recently resigned from his position at Google. His advancing age must have played a role in his resignation, but his reason is quite interesting. He predicts that the AI technologies being developed will lead to disinformation and that many people who do jobs automated by AI will lose their jobs. Being able to articulate these risks more clearly without being a Google employee 3.

Different Reactions

Before my own comments and opinion on the letter, I will give the reaction of every political commentator in Turkey and even every citizen who follows the agenda: “The timing is significant. But why now?” There is no harm in asking this question and being skeptical. Indeed, in the last decade, when deep learning has become popular, data-driven systems have become part of commercial products in many fields without adequately questioning the potential risks. For example, facial recognition systems perform differently on the faces of people from different ethnic groups. The fact that we can’t unlock the screen of the phone we paid the same money for with facial recognition just because of our skin color seems simple, but it is a significant problem. After all, these algorithms are based on data sets collected from the internet. So it wasn’t hard to predict that deep learning-based facial recognition systems would be biased toward different groups.

Of course, the problem is not always with the data or the algorithms. Sometimes it’s the responsibility of the people who develop and use these tools. When a group of researchers from Harrisburg University in the US wrote a paper predicting whether a person was a criminal or not from facial images, hundreds of researchers around the world reacted, and their paper was rejected by the publisher (4). Unfortunately, Western public opinion, the research world, and academic social media do not show similar sensitivity in all cases. Again, let’s continue with the hundred examples. At the end of 2020, The Washington Post reported that Huawei had developed an Uyghur recognition system in China based on facial images and that it was being used in security videos (5). This was an important achievement in terms of journalism. As you can imagine, identifying people’s race from a security camera does not have a very humanitarian purpose. This technology was a tool of genocide and was used to imprison people in concentration camps. Now, the same company has dozens of research centers in Europe and continues to research and develop on similar topics. The point I want to draw attention to here is that some risks and dangers get a lot of attention while others are ignored.

There are similar examples on different topics. For example, Geoffrey Hinton, who is voicing these dangers today, said in 2016, “We should stop training radiologists. It’s clear that in five years, deep learning will outperform radiologists,” and this resonated. On the contrary, every day shows us the importance of human and AI collaboration in medical imaging. AI radiologists and pathologists should understand these technologies and be able to interpret the results of algorithms on test images or electronic patient records but remain the final decision-makers. Unfortunately, there are risks associated with data-driven deep learning systems in autonomous vehicles, the defense industry, and many other areas. These have been known for a long time, but somehow the research and industry world has ignored them to get astronomical shares of the pie in economies, and they have created hype.

Now, the risks have become more visible in different ways, such as millions of people experimenting with big language models. However, at this point, a hasty ban or imposition without open discussion will only widen the gap between a few actors who are already dividing all the gains between them and the rest of the world.

The Challenge of Audit

As the letter suggests, it is difficult for all companies and research organizations globally to agree not to develop large language models. While many companies are training large AI models using cloud systems, plenty of companies can do this on their own. Those in the Western world doing research and development in these areas could take advantage of this interim period and outsource some of the work to other shell companies.

The issue concerns more than an interim period (or ban), which would be impossible to implement even in the US. Focusing on existing problems and discussing these risks and dangers with the masses can help us get out of the hype and onto the more realistic ground. Today, there are national and international standards for everything from charger cables to sockets, electronics to building technologies, and even agriculture and animal husbandry. In the same way, discussing such issues can accelerate the establishment of specific quality standards in AI-based products.

Different Criticisms

Here is a sentence from the letter: “AI research and development must be refocused on making today’s powerful, state-of-the-art systems more accurate, secure, interpretable, transparent, robust, adaptive, reliable, and faithful.”

Accuracy has become the primary goal and performance measure of AI systems. Perhaps it has become more complex with large language models and companies aiming for “artificial general intelligence”. We expect the same AI system to write program code, answer a scientific question or even develop a drug, or, as we tried in the previous article, write a technical paper on architecture. The systems developed should have an engineering purpose. For language models, however, there is not just one purpose but perhaps hundreds. This makes it impossible to test the accuracy of systems designed for general use in these tasks.

Securing models that are so difficult to test for accuracy is even more difficult. Maybe we don’t know what data sets models like ChatGPT are trained on. The content we ask questions about when we use them may also be in the training sets, and because we don’t know this, we may find the answers fascinating. Even if we know all the details about the data sets and how the models are trained, interpretability, transparency, and robustness are more difficult problems to solve. Systems with millions of parameters do not produce and explain these texts in logical processes that we humans can understand.

Earlier, when we talked about OpenAI’s goals, we mentioned adaptation. The targeting of adaptation and artificial general intelligence is another point of criticism. Being loyal is another concept we don’t understand and how it can be measured. To whom and according to what?

On top of all this, artificial intelligence systems based on images, videos and text use all these texts regardless of their copyright status. For example, if I want to use a photo taken by a photojournalist from a news agency in this article. If the license of the photo does not allow me to do so, I have to either contact them and ask for permission, or pay the usage fee and buy it. Even in the use of such a simple image, the boundaries of ownership are very clearly defined in law. Large language models, systems that produce images and videos, and moreover, do not pay attention to the rights of use of training sets. In such a situation, whose work is a text or a picture produced? Should we consider this plagiarism? Is there a copyright of the people whose data is used in the training set? There are dozens of questions like these that experts from different disciplines need to think about.

The criticisms and debates are not limited to these. Another point of divergence is the so-called long-termism, which researchers such as Emily M. Bender and Timnit Gebru draw attention to, and which sometimes veers towards science fiction. In fact, FLI was founded and funded by people with this ideology. In short, long-termism can be defined as realizing the long-term goals and potential of humanity, thereby serving the future of humanity in general. This relates to a number of issues beyond artificial intelligence, such as the climate crisis, and is better dealt with in a separate article.

As with many other issues, reading the comments and criticisms of different people on social media provides a multidimensional perspective. In fact, making connections with seemingly unrelated issues can make you say “I never thought of that”. One example is the tweet (6) by Yann LeCun, a professor at New York University and a prominent figure in deep learning who also did research for Meta (Facebook) for many years, and the subsequent responses by Lebanese-American author Nassim Nicholas Taleb and historian Emrah Safa Gürkan.

“The Ottoman Empire restricted the spread of printed books for fear of religious and political turmoil. As a result, it missed the Age of Enlightenment, lost its dominant position in science and technology, and ultimately lost its economic and military influence.”

This tweet by Yann LeCun is a commentary on the letter. Indeed, stopping the work on artificial intelligence today is very similar to banning the printing press. Emrah Safa Gürkan’s response is that there are not enough sources indicating that the Ottoman Empire banned the printing press, and that it was more of a cultural resistance, perhaps the public’s lack of interest in reading cheap books. In fact, until now I had never thought about whether the story of the banning of the printing press, which I had been taught in schools and had heard since I was a child, was true or not. When I did some research, I found some history articles that supported Emrah Safa Gürkan’s view. This means that oversimplifying many issues leads us to look at them from a narrower perspective and to make subjective evaluations. The same applies to current debates.

In this article, I’ve summarized the open letter published by The Future of Life Institute (FLI), the issues discussed in general terms, and expressed my own thoughts. The applications of artificial intelligence in everyday life is a complex topic that involves different disciplines such as psychology, sociology, ethics and law. In most of the points I have briefly mentioned, it is very difficult to talk about an absolute directness. It is very important to raise public awareness and make these issues known and discussed. In future articles, I will try to address these issues in more detail through different examples.
